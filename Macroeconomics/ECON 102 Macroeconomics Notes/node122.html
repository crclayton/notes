Monetary Transmission Mechanism

There is a relationship between the demand and supply of money and aggregate demand. In essence, by changing the money supply we can shift aggregate expenditure, real GDP, in the short run. This is called the monetary transmission mechanism.

This occurs in three stages...

  1. Change in demand/money supply change the interest rate

    Suppose there is an increase in the money supply or a decrease in the money demanded. This creates an excess supply of money.

    So since everyone has extra money, everyone starts buying more bonds, and by supply and demand the price of bonds increases.

    The increase in the price of bonds causes a decrease in the interest rate.

  2. Change in the interest rate changes aggregate expenditure

    Continuing from above, suppose the interest rate decreases. This will lead to more investment expenditure (recall by this we mean investment in mortgages and cars and other things purchased on credit, not investment in accounts), so 8#8 increases. Similarly, consumption expenditure 7#7 increases.

    On top of 8#8 and 7#7 increasing, we also get 10#10 increasing. This is because a decrease in interest rates means investors will sell Canadian bonds and buy foreign bonds instead. This means Canadian dollars will decrease in desirability relative to other currencies, so it depreciates in value.

    This means that Canadian goods are more cheap relative to foreign goods, so imports decrease and exports rise, thus 10#10 increases. This is the open-economy modification.

    All of these changes shift aggregate expenditure up.

  3. Change in AE shift the AD curve

    A shift upward in the AE curved caused by something other than a change in price level leads to a rightward shift in the AD curve.

    So finally, this rightward shifts increases the short-run real GDP and the price level.

So that's quite a process but let's recap.
