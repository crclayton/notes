Resistances, Reactances, and Impedances

So there are voltages and currents. We're two-thirds of the way there, we just have to talk about resistances now. But your DC circuits teacher lied to you, Ohms law isn't 7#7, it's 8#8. We're no longer just talking about resistances. When we incorporate capacitors and inductors in AC, we're talking about impedances. An impedance, 9#9 is also a vector. Though we typically don't treat it like a magnitude and an angle, as we do with voltages and currents. Instead, it's broken up into its components. Once component, which we'll treat as the horizontal axis, is the resistance, 10#10. That's just the value from the resistor in the branch. The other component is reactance, 11#11.

For an inductor, the reactance is 12#12. For a capacitor the reactance is 13#13, which includes those frequencies I said we would ignore.

There's one thing I've been putting off mentioning. So these values are vectors right, so one component is in the horizontal direction, usually called 14#14, and one component is in the vertical direction, usually called 15#15. But instead of writing our vectors as 16#16, we're going to imagine them as in the complex plain, so we'll treat them as complex numbers. The x-axis is the real component, the y-axis is the imaginary component. And because electrical engineers already decided to use 17#17 to represent current (which sidenote, stands for intensity of current), we will instead use 18#18 as the imaginary number. So our vector instead becomes a complex number, 19#19

All that to say the impedance vector is made up of the resistance in the branch as the real component, and the reactance of the branch as the imaginary component, 20#20
